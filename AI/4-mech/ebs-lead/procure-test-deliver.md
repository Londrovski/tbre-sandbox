Make it real — get the parts ordered in time and on budget, built safely, and tested on the physical car, including the dynamic stop test it has to pass.

## Owns
- Ordering the parts
- Building the system
- Testing it — on the bench, then on the car

## Delivers
- Parts ordered on time and on budget, with the cost info flowing to Finance
- The system bench-tested early, before it goes on the car
- The full system tested on the physical car — including the dynamic emergency-stop test
- Pressure across the whole team to build and test as soon as possible

## What's involved
A safety system only counts once it's built, on the car, and proven to stop it. This responsibility owns the path from design to a tested system: ordering early (watching the long-lead and single-source parts), giving Finance the cost and supplier information to order on time and on budget, building the pneumatic/hydraulic system safely, bench-testing each circuit in isolation, then integrating and validating on the car up to the dynamic stop test the scrutineers require. It's the delivery-discipline half of the role — and for a safety system, the testing is the whole point.

## This year (context, not structure)
- The bill of materials is already specced (mostly off-the-shelf, ~£1.3k) in the **EBS technical report** — a starting point, not a locked order. Long-lead and single-source items (the solenoid valve and the certified gas canister) are the supply risks: order early and carry a spare canister.
- **Nothing is built yet.** Manufacture, isolated bench testing, then on-car integration and the dynamic stop test (accelerate past ~40 km/h, then trigger an emergency stop) are the next steps — targeted at the summer post-season window.
- Pneumatics handling is a safety job in itself: regulator mounted on the tank, clean air, heat isolation, pressure checks, and the canister's fixed replacement interval.
- **James** can be boots-on-the-ground in Bath over summer for build, fitting and fault-finding.
